Are you mobile-friendly?

Sept. 14, 2015

Mobile-friendly is nothing new, but it just got more important.

As smart phones become more and more popular, an increasingly significant portion of searches are made from mobile devices. They can’t be considered second tier. We need one tier, that "responds to user's behavior and environment based on screen size, platform and orientation."

Google has been encouraging mobile friendliness and responsive design to get there since at least 2012. And in April 2015, when Google rolled out their mobile-friendly update, they made a change that everyone designing for the web needs to know.

Now, when searching from a mobile device, Google ranks any site that is not mobile-friendly lower on their search results!

Google has a pile of reasons for doing this. For some time now, companies have been making multiple sites for different devices. A site for mobile (m), a site for tablets (t), and the desktop site. All separate, but using the same or similar info, all needing to be updated individually.

How exhausting!

All these branches are messy and make the web a less unified place. And no one likes that lag when being redirected from desktop site to mobile site is a significant distraction for users.

With Google's latest announcement the path is clear. It's time to get responsive.

You can check to see if your site meets their demands easily enough.
Here
Some tips:
  • responsive design is your friend, Google specifically recommends "using RWD over other design patterns”. (But if you are feeling bossed around by Google, don’t get too upset, responsive design is just a suggestion.) It’s mobile- friendly that matters right now.
  • go mobile first - Mobile-first design prioritizes use for mobile users and works it way up to larger monitors and greater complexity.
  • website must load quickly (seriously quickly-under a second). They provide this link to see how you measure up: Here
There are, of course, some downsides:
  • it takes longer to design for so many more variables, even though it saves time in the long run
  • lack of support from older browsers
  • while the big guys can, and are responsive, smaller companies are the ones who are most likely to be negatively impacted by the change since they may not have the resources to do a redesign from scratch.
  • resizing images and content must be done on the server size.
  • it’s tricky to break up content for different devices without using different code.
